MERV Ratings Explained — What Filter Rating Do You Actually Need?

Last updated April 2026 · 10 min read

MERV stands for Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value — a standard created by ASHRAE that rates how effectively an air filter captures particles of different sizes.

The scale runs from 1 to 20. For residential HVAC systems, you'll typically choose between MERV 5 and MERV 16. Here's what each range actually means, and which one your home probably needs. Once you know your MERV, check our best filters guide for specific product picks.

MERV 1–4: Basic Protection

MERV
1–4

These are the flat fiberglass panel filters you see at hardware stores for $1–$3. They catch large particles — carpet fibers, pollen, and large dust bunnies — but that's about it. Their primary job is protecting your HVAC equipment, not improving air quality.

Who should use them: Vacant properties, unoccupied spaces, or as a pre-filter. Not recommended as a sole filter in a lived-in home.

Airflow impact: Minimal. These are basically open mesh.

MERV 5–8: The Standard

MERV
5–8

This is what most homes run, and for good reason. MERV 8 in particular hits a sweet spot: it captures dust, pollen, mold spores, and large pet dander particles without restricting airflow in any residential system.

Who should use them: Homes without allergy sufferers, pet-free homes, rental properties, vacation homes. MERV 8 is the minimum we recommend for any occupied home.

Airflow impact: Very low. Compatible with all residential HVAC systems.

MERV 9–12: Enhanced Filtration

MERV
9–12

This is our recommended range for most homes, especially if you have pets or mild allergies. MERV 11 and 12 catch significantly more fine dust, pet dander, and even some auto emissions. The Filtrete 1500 (MERV 12) and Nordic Pure MERV 12 are both excellent choices here.

Who should use them: Homes with pets, mild to moderate allergies, dusty environments, or anyone who wants noticeably cleaner air.

Airflow impact: Low to moderate. Works fine with most systems built in the last 20 years.

Our picks: Factor Filter MERV 11 (best overall — factory-direct, Made in USA), Filtrete 1500 (best retail), and Nordic Pure MERV 12 (best value on Amazon).

MERV 13–16: Maximum Residential Filtration

MERV
13–16

These are the heavy hitters. MERV 13 is the highest rating the CDC and ASHRAE recommend for typical residential use. It captures particles down to 0.3 microns — including bacteria, smoke, sneeze droplets, and many virus carriers.

Who should use them: Severe allergy or asthma sufferers, immunocompromised individuals, homes near wildfires or heavy traffic. See our allergy filter guide for specific picks.

Airflow impact: Moderate to significant. You must check your system's capabilities. Variable-speed blowers handle high-MERV filters much better than single-speed systems.

Our pick: Factor Filter MERV 13 — factory-direct, Made in USA, every standard size. Also: Filtrete 2200 (MERV 13) at retail. For custom sizes, BowTie Filters offers premium MERV 13 options.

Important: Running a MERV 13+ filter on a system that can't handle it is worse than running a MERV 8. Restricted airflow makes your system work harder, increases energy costs, and can cause damage. When in doubt, stick with MERV 11 or 12.

MERV vs. FPR vs. MPR

You'll see three rating systems in stores:

  • MERV (industry standard) — ASHRAE-certified, tested by third parties. This is the one that matters.
  • FPR (Filter Performance Rating) — Home Depot's proprietary scale (1–10). Roughly correlates to MERV but isn't independently verified.
  • MPR (Microparticle Performance Rating) — 3M/Filtrete's proprietary system. MPR 1500 ≈ MERV 12, MPR 2200 ≈ MERV 13.

When in doubt, use MERV. It's the only standard that's universally comparable across brands.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a MERV 13 filter in my home?

Probably, if your system was built in the last 15–20 years and has a variable-speed blower. Older single-speed systems may struggle with the increased airflow resistance. Check your manual or ask your HVAC technician.

Is MERV 8 good enough?

For homes without pets or allergy sufferers, absolutely. MERV 8 catches the vast majority of common household particles and has zero risk of airflow problems. It's the baseline we recommend for every home.

What about HEPA filters for furnaces?

True HEPA filters are MERV 17–20. They can't be used in standard residential HVAC systems — the airflow restriction would destroy your equipment. HEPA filtration in homes is done through standalone portable units. See our allergy filter guide for more.

What is the difference between MERV, FPR, and MPR?

MERV is the industry standard set by ASHRAE. FPR is Home Depot's proprietary scale (1–10). MPR is 3M/Filtrete's system. MERV is the only universally comparable standard across brands.

Do higher MERV filters cost more to run?

Slightly. Higher MERV = more airflow resistance = your blower works a bit harder. The difference between MERV 8 and MERV 12 is typically $2–$5/month in energy costs. The jump from MERV 12 to 13 is where it can start to add up on older systems.

Bottom Line: For most homes, MERV 11 or 12 is the goldilocks zone — significantly better air quality than MERV 8, without the airflow concerns of MERV 13+. Ready to buy? See our top filter picks.
