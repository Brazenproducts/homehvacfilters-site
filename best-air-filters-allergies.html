If you or someone in your home suffers from allergies or asthma, your HVAC filter is one of the most impactful (and cheapest) things you can upgrade. The right filter captures pollen, pet dander, dust mite debris, and mold spores before they circulate through your home.

Here's what actually works, what doesn't, and which filters we recommend. Not sure what MERV ratings mean? Start there first.

What Allergy Sufferers Should Look For

  • MERV 11 minimum, MERV 13 ideal — This captures the particle sizes that trigger most allergic reactions (1–3 microns for dust mite debris, 10–100 microns for pollen, 2–20 microns for mold spores).
  • Electrostatic charge — Filters with electrostatically charged media (like Filtrete) attract and hold particles more effectively than purely mechanical filtration.
  • Proper fit — Gaps around the filter let unfiltered air bypass. Make sure you have the right size.
  • Regular changes — A loaded filter releases allergens back into the air. Change every 45–60 days during allergy season.

Tips for Allergy Sufferers

  • Change filters every 45–60 days during allergy season, not the standard 90. See our maintenance guide.
  • Run the fan continuously during high-pollen days (set thermostat fan to "ON" instead of "AUTO"). This keeps air circulating through the filter even when heating/cooling isn't active.
  • Seal the filter slot. Use foam weatherstripping around the filter frame to eliminate bypass air.
  • Keep windows closed during high-pollen days and let your HVAC filter do the work.
  • Vacuum with a HEPA-filter vacuum to reduce the particle load your HVAC filter has to handle.
  • Consider a portable HEPA purifier for the bedroom in addition to your furnace filter.

Frequently Asked Questions

What MERV rating is best for allergies?

MERV 13 is the gold standard for allergy sufferers. It captures particles down to 0.3 microns, including pollen, mold spores, pet dander, dust mite debris, and some bacteria. MERV 11–12 is a good compromise if your system can't handle MERV 13.

Can I put a HEPA filter in my furnace?

No. True HEPA filters (MERV 17–20) are too restrictive for residential HVAC systems and would damage your equipment. For whole-home HEPA filtration, you need a bypass HEPA system installed by an HVAC professional. For room-level HEPA, use a standalone portable air purifier.

Do air filters help with seasonal allergies?

Yes. A MERV 11+ filter captures pollen, mold spores, and other seasonal allergens. Combined with keeping windows closed during high-pollen days, a good HVAC filter significantly reduces indoor allergen levels.

How often should allergy sufferers change their HVAC filter?

Every 45–60 days for 1-inch filters. During peak allergy season (spring and fall), check monthly. A loaded filter can release trapped allergens back into the air. See our full change schedule guide.
